Analyzing the Potential Berberine Benefits for Health and Wellness: What Science Says

Managing weight is a growing concern worldwide, with recent estimates showing nearly 43% of adults on the planet could be considered overweight. This has sparked a massive market for weight loss solutions. Among the countless products available, berberine supplements have emerged as a potential ally.

As weight loss is tied to general health, by shedding pounds, individuals carrying excess weight can not only improve their confidence but also lower the risk for certain chronic diseases and cardiovascular concerns. Of course, it's also critical to remember that how you lose weight also matters.

There are a lot of claims that taking berberine may help with many things, ranging from weight loss to lowering blood sugar levels. So, what do scientific studies suggest berberine supplementation may do for you, and are there any risks?

Key takeaways:
  • Some studies suggest that berberine may support metabolic health, but further studies are needed to better understand berberine’s impact on overall health.
  • Using berberine may support weight loss, lower LDL and total cholesterol levels, and help manage blood glucose levels.
  • The extent of weight loss associated with berberine supplements appears to be relatively modest (i.e., about 2 kg or 4.4 lbs on average).
  • It's unclear whether berberine provides any long-term cardiovascular benefits.
  • Consulting with a healthcare provider can help you determine whether adding berberine may be beneficial and help ensure it can be used safely based on your individual needs.

What is berberine?

Berberine is a natural compound found in the stems, bark, and roots of certain plants. Berberine has a long history in traditional Chinese medicine as a component of an herbal remedy derived from plants like Coptis chinensis, a flowering plant species native to China.

Some of the potential benefits touted for berberine supplements have included support for:

  • Weight loss
  • Blood sugar control
  • Managing polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
  • Lowering high LDL cholesterol, triglyceride levels, and heart disease risk
  • Liver health

We'll explore some of the research in more detail to help determine the reliability of these claims. First, let's look at berberine's proposed effects on the body.

What does berberine do in the body?

Berberine's potential mechanisms of action are not fully understood. Some of its effects seem to come from promoting the activity of AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), an important enzyme involved in regulating cellular energy metabolism. It also affects potassium channels in the pancreas, causing increased insulin secretion and, ultimately, more glucose to be taken into cells.

Essentially, berberine may help regulate cell metabolism. These kinds of actions may have several important effects, including:

  • Improved blood sugar regulation due to increased insulin secretion, increased glucose uptake from the bloodstream into cells, and reduced glucose production in the liver. Berberine may also improve insulin sensitivity. These effects can combine to lower blood sugar levels.
  • Improved regulation of blood lipid levels, including decreased LDL cholesterol and triglyceride levels. These effects may help reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease.

Overall, these mechanisms could ultimately be helpful for lowering body mass index, LDL cholesterol, heightened blood pressure, or managing insulin resistance.

However, it's important to understand the distinction between a proposed mechanism and actual real-world results. So, what do clinical trials tell us specifically about the effects of taking berberine?

Exploring berberine's potential health benefits

While berberine has gained a lot of attention on social media platforms, examining the scientific evidence can help distinguish the hype from reality. As a PharmD, making evidence-based recommendations is central to my practice.

Extensive research is needed before broad clinical recommendations can be made, and this research often develops over the span of years or even decades to ensure treatments are both safe and effective. Let's explore what the current research suggests about using berberine in more detail.

Weight loss

Terms like ‘nature's Ozempic’ have been used to describe berberine, but it's important to put this into perspective. Research suggests that clinically significant weight loss can be defined as a loss of about 5% of initial body weight. When individuals considered overweight or obese lose 5% or more of their body weight, the likelihood of beneficial effects like lowering high blood pressure or reducing cardiovascular risk factors becomes higher.

Clinical studies of berberine related to its ability to help people lose weight show modest (and often inconsistent) results. A systematic review of randomized controlled trials shows an average weight loss of about 2 kg (4.4 lbs). Although this was statistically significant, indicating that berberine may aid in weight loss, the current data related to the amount lost doesn't necessarily mean that this loss is clinically significant.

More studies would be needed to provide a better perspective on berberine's weight loss potential. Ultimately, berberine, like other supplements marketed for weight loss, must be considered a complement to, rather than a replacement for, proven weight management strategies like balanced nutrition and consistent physical activity.

Blood sugar control

Berberine's use in traditional Chinese medicine has partially hinged on blood sugar management, and this is one of the areas where the evidence for berberine may be the strongest.

Research has shown that glucose and HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin, an important measure of blood glucose levels over time) levels are lowered by berberine. However, the results have been somewhat inconsistent, particularly for fasting blood glucose levels.

Some researchers have pointed out that improvements seem to be more pronounced when individual factors like HbA1c are already elevated, suggesting berberine's activity lies specifically in response to high blood glucose levels. So, if blood sugar levels are already high, berberine may be more effective in promoting insulin release.

Initial studies are promising, but additional research would be helpful in identifying who may be most likely to benefit from berberine based on individual needs and circumstances.

Disclaimer
It's critical to talk with your healthcare providers before making significant changes like starting a berberine supplement. For individuals already using diabetes medications, it's important to be cautious when adding new supplements that can affect blood sugar levels. Particularly because of berberine's proposed mechanism of increased insulin release, individuals who are already using insulin or an insulin secretagogue (e.g., sulfonylureas like glipizide) may be at a higher risk of developing hypoglycemia or dangerously low blood sugar levels.

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)

A standard of care for managing PCOS is metformin, which is thought to help with symptoms by improving insulin sensitivity. Berberine may also help improve insulin sensitivity, so it may logically also help with PCOS. For individuals with a PCOS diagnosis, metabolic markers like blood sugar and LDL cholesterol levels are important to monitor, and the disease can also impact fertility due to reproductive hormone imbalances.

A comprehensive review of clinical trials conducted in China has explored the effects of berberine on metabolic markers and fertility. Overall, it seems that berberine may help:

  • Promote ovulation and increase pregnancy rates, especially when paired with IVF (in vitro fertilization); however, the number of live births remained unchanged
  • Lower blood sugar levels
  • Decrease LDL cholesterol levels

While the results were promising, it's important to note that there was a lot of variability between the individual trials. This makes it harder to predict whether berberine may be beneficial for larger groups of people managing PCOS. Ultimately, more research would be needed to confirm the results.

Always work with your healthcare provider to see if berberine is a safe option for you, and follow up regularly to ensure your approach is working well and remains safe over time.

Heart health

Evaluating berberine's potential heart health benefits is tricky because its effects on actual heart disease rates haven’t been studied. This is why berberine, like other over-the-counter supplements, cannot be intended to directly treat or prevent heart disease.

However, risk factors for heart disease are well established, and some studies explore berberine's impact on causal factors of CVD, like high LDL cholesterol levels. Generally, berberine seems to:

  • Decrease total cholesterol
  • Decrease LDL cholesterol (low-density lipoprotein cholesterol)

These data suggest that berberine ‘supports heart health’ but doesn't tell us how much of an impact that support might have on heart disease rates. Without this information, deciding whether the benefits of adding the supplement will likely outweigh any potential risks of side effects or interactions is more challenging.

The bottom line is that established therapies that work are already available. If you are considered at high risk of heart disease, your healthcare provider is very likely to prescribe a statin. Regardless of your approach, your success still hinges heavily on diet and lifestyle choices. This is also the case if your goal is to prevent risk factors from developing in the first place.

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD)

Fatty liver disease can develop alongside weight gain as the body stores excess fat in the liver. NAFLD is reversible, but if untreated, it can lead to liver damage over time. If not addressed, this damage increases the risk of developing cirrhosis or liver cancer.

Similar to heart health, we don't really have data about berberine and whether it directly lowers the risk of cirrhosis or liver cancer. However, improved LDL cholesterol levels may provide support, and some studies also track liver enzymes like AST and ALT (important markers that signify liver damage when elevated).

For individuals managing NAFLD, liver enzyme levels improved while using berberine. Many of the studies were short-term and relatively small, but the initial results suggest there may be some early promise for berberine's role as an addition to current treatments of NAFLD. Further studies will be needed before reaching a broader medical consensus about berberine’s potential role in managing NAFLD.

Like other conditions caused by metabolic changes, diet and lifestyle remain critical for reversing the progression of NAFLD and preventing liver damage, regardless of whether other treatments or supplements are used.

Berberine supplements: what to consider

If you're considering a berberine supplement, keeping a few things in mind is important.

Most studies suggest that doses between 500 to 1,000 mg daily are likely safe. Some studies use doses up to 1,500 mg a day. Most studies ended after several months. Since we don't know about long-term effects, it's important to work with your healthcare provider to help ensure you stay on the right track.

If a berberine supplement is recommended, it's helpful to remember that not all products are created equal. Look for products that:

  • Clearly list ingredients (of particular importance for individuals with allergies or who follow a vegan lifestyle, etc.)
  • Are GMP-certified, implying a commitment to quality manufacturing
  • Undergo independent third-party testing, verifying that what's on the label matches what's actually in the bottle

Supplements are not regulated like medications. Choosing a quality product and discussing it with your healthcare provider can help ensure that you're making a safe, informed choice for your health.

First-hand experience with berberine

Drawing upon the experiences of others can offer some perspective, particularly when clinical research is limited. However, it’s important to note that individual experiences may vary.

In my day-to-day practice as a PharmD, I’ve spoken with a few individuals who have taken berberine for an extended period of time, and their experiences may offer some insight. All of them were taking berberine to support weight loss. None of them were using berberine specifically as a means to control blood sugar or cholesterol levels. All of them have mentioned initial gastrointestinal side effects like nausea or loose stools. These have gotten better as the body adjusted to the supplementation for some, but for others, the symptoms were bothersome enough for them to stop using berberine.

One person reports cycling berberine over time (e.g., three months on and two months off before starting over) at the advice of their healthcare provider. No studies suggest this approach is safer or more effective, so it’s important to check in with a healthcare professional regularly to ensure your approach is still the best plan for you.

One individual reported significant weight loss (about 40 lbs) when paired with a consistent diet and exercise. Limiting processed foods in her diet while focusing on whole foods and staying physically active was also a prominent part of her plan.

Safety and side effects

Across all current studies, berberine has been considered well-tolerated overall. The most common side effects reported in studies include:

It's critical to talk with your healthcare providers if you are pregnant or plan to become pregnant. Berberine crosses the placenta and thus may have a harmful effect for the fetus.

It's also important to note that berberine can interact with certain medications by affecting CYP3A4 liver enzymes. These enzymes process many medications, and when mixed with berberine, which is proposed to be an inhibitor of CYP3A4 action, may cause medication levels to increase in the plasma, making side effects more likely.

Conclusion

Berberine shows some promise in several areas of health, particularly those impacted by metabolic changes. While social media buzz has increased its popularity, scientific evidence suggests relatively modest benefits, and there is no medical consensus on berberine use. It's important to talk with your healthcare providers and mention all of your current treatments to ensure a new supplement can be used safely.

FAQ

Why don't doctors prescribe berberine?

Doctors do not prescribe Berberine because there is no medical consensus on its use. It’s available over-the-counter, but it’s still vital to talk with your doctor to ensure adding it to your routine will be safe.

Are there any foods that contain berberine?

Some foods do contain berberine, including barberries, tree turmeric, and goldthread.

Can you take berberine every day?

Berberine has been considered safe for most individuals when used daily for periods of up to three months. The safety of continuing to take berberine daily for longer periods is unclear.
